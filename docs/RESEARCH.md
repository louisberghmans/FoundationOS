# Product framework and research

Foundation OS translates established impact and grantmaking ideas into a workflow small enough for a family to use consistently.

## 1. Define purpose as an outcome system

A useful foundation brief has six levels:

| Level | Question | Stored in Foundation OS |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Mission | What lasting change are we here to help create? | One shared mission statement |
| Scope | For whom, where, and within which themes? | Geography and focus areas |
| Objective | What observable outcome should exist by when? | Outcome title and deadline |
| Metric | What would count as progress? | Metric, unit, baseline/current value, target |
| Thesis | Why should this kind of work cause that outcome? | Plain-language causal thesis |
| Portfolio contribution | How might each grant move the metric? | Linked objective and estimated contribution |

This is a compact Theory of Change. The UK Government’s current evaluation guidance describes a causal chain from inputs through outputs to outcomes, supported by evidence and explicit assumptions rather than assertion. Its Magenta Book also emphasizes mechanisms, risks, and contextual factors. Foundation OS therefore treats the thesis and evidence level as first-class information, not footnotes. ([Green Book 2026](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/the-green-book-appraisal-and-evaluation-in-central-government/the-green-book-2026), [Magenta Book](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/the-magenta-book/magenta-book-central-government-guidance-on-evaluation-html))

For the example water objective, the app uses people with sustained safe-water access rather than wells installed. That aligns the objective with UN Sustainable Development Goal target 6.1 and its safely managed drinking-water indicator. ([UN SDG 6](https://sdgs.un.org/goals/goal6))

### Recommended objective template

> By **[date]**, **[population]** in **[place]** will experience **[measurable outcome]**, moving from **[baseline]** to **[target]**. We believe **[intervention class]** can contribute because **[evidence-backed causal thesis]**. We will watch **[assumption or risk]**.

The wording deliberately says “contribute.” A family grant is rarely the only cause of a social outcome.

## 2. Assess opportunities without a false precision score

The diligence model combines four decision inputs:

1. **Outcome fit:** relevance to an agreed objective, geography, and population.
2. **Expected value:** estimated contribution, total cost, cost per outcome where comparable, and confirmed room for more funding.
3. **Confidence:** evidence strength, delivery track record, transparency, and the important assumptions that could break.
4. **Responsibility:** organizational health, safeguarding, conflicts, financial controls, local legitimacy, and downside risk.

GiveWell publicly describes effectiveness, cost-effectiveness, room for more funding, and transparency as core criteria. It also warns that cost-effectiveness estimates are rough and should not be the sole basis of a decision. The interface follows that spirit: show the inputs and uncertainties together instead of collapsing them into a single seductive number. ([GiveWell process](https://www.givewell.org/how-we-work), [cost-effectiveness limitations](https://www.givewell.org/how-we-work/our-criteria/cost-effectiveness/cost-effectiveness-models))

## 3. Use a stage-gated grant workflow

The v1 board uses six stages:

| Stage | Exit condition |
| --- | --- |
| Intake | Basic fit, request, organization, and objective link are recorded |
| Due diligence | Evidence, funding gap, risk, references, budget, and conflicts are understood |
| Decision | The brief is complete and every eligible member has recorded a vote |
| Active grant | Agreement, payment, reporting expectations, and steward are clear |
| Review | Results and learning are assessed against the original thesis |
| Complete | Final record is preserved and outcome contribution is retained |

The review is structured around the OECD DAC’s six complementary lenses: **relevance, coherence, effectiveness, efficiency, impact, and sustainability**. OECD explicitly advises applying the criteria thoughtfully and according to the evaluation’s purpose, so the roadmap avoids a mandatory heavyweight questionnaire for every small grant. ([OECD DAC guidance](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/applying-evaluation-criteria-thoughtfully_543e84ed-en.html))

## 4. Make family governance explicit

The user’s family decides unanimously. The product represents this as a rule with visible member-by-member state: pending, approve, or concern. A concern prevents progression and should carry a note in the production release.

After approval, one steward becomes accountable for the relationship and follow-up; this is not exclusive control. The family retains the complete record and can see overdue actions.

A production governance model also needs conflict disclosure and recusal. The Council on Foundations recommends written rules requiring board members and executives to disclose ties that could create bias around a grant or investment. This is included in the post-v1 roadmap because recusal changes the eligible unanimity denominator and requires a durable audit log. ([Council on Foundations](https://cof.org/content/conflicts-interest-key-issues-foundations))

## 5. Separate accountability from learning

Every funded project should answer two different questions:

- **Accountability:** Were funds used as agreed, were outputs delivered, and were safeguards followed?
- **Learning:** Did the expected pathway hold, what changed for people, what else influenced the result, and what should the family do differently?

The first protects the foundation. The second makes future giving better. Reviews should retain negative or unexpected results rather than quietly converting them into a lower progress number.

## What v1 intentionally does not claim

- Estimated project contribution is not causal attribution.
- A “strong evidence” badge is demonstration data, not an independent rating.
- Local browser persistence is not suitable for confidential foundation records.
- The example framework is operating guidance, not tax or legal advice for a particular jurisdiction.
